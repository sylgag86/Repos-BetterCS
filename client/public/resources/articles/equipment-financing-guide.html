Equipment Financing for Small Business: Everything You Need to Know

March 2, 202616 min read

Equipment is one of the biggest cost centers for many small businesses, and in 2026, replacement cycles are getting shorter while purchase prices remain elevated. Whether you run a trucking company buying trailers, a restaurant upgrading kitchen systems, or a medical office adding diagnostic tools, financing can preserve cash while still letting you grow. The challenge is choosing the right structure and avoiding expensive terms hidden behind "low monthly payment" marketing.

This guide explains how equipment financing works, when to lease versus buy, what lenders review, and how to compare offers from providers such as Balboa Capital, Crest Capital, and Taycor Financial. You'll also see where it fits relative to other products like a business line of credit and category-focused options on Better Capital Solutions.

What is equipment financing?

Equipment financing is funding specifically used to acquire business equipment. The equipment itself usually serves as collateral, which can make approvals easier than unsecured financing. Common assets include vehicles, construction equipment, manufacturing machinery, POS systems, dental and medical devices, office hardware, and specialized software-hardware bundles.

Most transactions fall into two buckets:

  • Equipment loan: You own the asset (or are clearly moving toward ownership) while making installment payments.
  • Equipment lease: You pay for use over time and either return, renew, or purchase at the end depending on lease type.

Lease vs loan: how to choose the right structure

When a loan usually makes more sense

Loans are often better when the equipment has a long useful life, you expect to keep it well past the payoff date, and you want full control over maintenance schedules and disposition. Owning can also reduce long-run cost if you operate the asset efficiently and avoid frequent replacement.

When leasing can be smarter

Leasing can be attractive when technology becomes outdated quickly, when upfront cash is tight, or when you prefer lower monthly commitments in early growth phases. It may also simplify replacement cycles in industries where equipment drives customer perception and must stay current.

Key lease types

  • $1 buyout lease: Similar to financing ownership; often higher monthly payments, nominal purchase at end.
  • 10% purchase option lease: Lower payment than $1 buyout, optional purchase for preset residual.
  • Fair market value lease: Lowest payment profile, but end-of-term buyout is based on market value.

What lenders evaluate in 2026

Business profile and time in operation

Startups can qualify, but established businesses with at least 1-2 years in operation generally receive stronger rates and longer terms. Newer businesses may need larger down payments or personal guarantees.

Revenue and cash flow

Lenders want to confirm that equipment payments fit comfortably into normal operating cash flow. Businesses with recurring contracts, diversified customer bases, and consistent deposits are usually viewed more favorably.

Credit and guarantor strength

Both business and personal credit may be considered. Higher scores can reduce cost, but many lenders still approve fair-credit profiles when revenue, collateral quality, and industry outlook are strong.

Equipment quality and resale value

Because the asset is collateral, make, model, age, condition, and market liquidity matter. New equipment often qualifies for better terms; used equipment can still be financed but usually faces tighter underwriting.

Top lenders to evaluate

Balboa Capital

Often considered for fast digital workflows and a broad range of equipment categories. Useful for businesses that want efficient processing and flexible ticket sizes.

Crest Capital

Commonly compared for straightforward structures and transparent documentation expectations. Many owners use Crest when they want predictable ownership-focused programs.

Taycor Financial

Known in the equipment space and often considered by businesses shopping multiple lease and finance structures. Compare term length, down payment requirements, and end-of-term provisions carefully.

How to compare offers beyond monthly payment

  • Total cost of ownership: Payment × term + fees + buyout + maintenance impact.
  • Useful life alignment: Match term length to expected economic use, not just lowest payment.
  • Residual and end-of-term rules: Understand exactly what happens at maturity.
  • Insurance and servicing requirements: These can materially affect all-in cost.
  • Prepayment language: Some agreements include full remaining payment obligations even if paid early.

Step-by-step process to secure equipment financing

1) Define production impact and ROI

Before you request funding, estimate how the equipment affects revenue, efficiency, labor cost, scrap rate, turnaround time, or customer capacity. Lenders and internal decision-makers respond better to measurable outcomes than generic "growth" language.

2) Get a detailed vendor quote

Include make/model, serial numbers if available, warranty details, installation, shipping, software licenses, taxes, and any training components. Incomplete quotes slow approvals.

3) Prepare underwriting package

  • Recent business bank statements
  • Year-to-date and prior-year financials
  • Business formation docs and ownership breakdown
  • Existing debt obligations
  • Equipment quote and vendor contact

4) Submit to a focused lender set

Apply with a small, strategic group aligned to your profile. If you need alternatives, review the core category page for equipment financing options and related resources in the BCS blog.

5) Negotiate structure, not just rate

Term, down payment, payment frequency, deferred first payment, seasonal adjustments, and end-of-term option can all be negotiated depending on your profile and the lender's appetite.

Tax and accounting considerations to review with your advisor

Equipment decisions affect taxes and financial statements. Depending on structure, businesses may benefit from depreciation treatment, expense timing, or cash-flow management advantages. Because rules vary by entity type and situation, involve your CPA before signing. The best financing offer on paper is not always the best after-tax outcome.

Common mistakes and how to avoid them

  • Overbuying capacity: Match equipment size to demand forecast, not best-case optimism.
  • Ignoring uptime risk: Factor service contract quality and parts availability.
  • No replacement plan: Build a lifecycle strategy before first payment starts.
  • Choosing lowest payment blindly: Low payment can hide high residual or restrictive terms.
  • Missing integration costs: Installation, training, compliance upgrades, and downtime matter.

When to pair equipment financing with other capital tools

Many businesses combine products: an equipment loan for long-life assets and a line of credit for short working-capital gaps. This separation prevents long-lived purchases from consuming liquidity needed for payroll, inventory, and receivables timing. If credit is tight, review broader pathways in bad credit funding categories and compare lenders that support operationally strong but credit-challenged businesses.

Industry-specific examples: how structure changes by business model

Construction and trades

Contractors often finance skid steers, excavators, lifts, and trucks that directly drive job capacity. Because utilization can swing with season and backlog, payment structures with seasonal flexibility may be valuable. For these operators, maintenance planning is as important as rate shopping: a machine down during peak season can erase financing savings quickly.

Healthcare and dental practices

Medical and dental equipment can generate high-margin services but requires strict compliance, calibration, and upgrade planning. Practices typically benefit from ownership-oriented structures when equipment is expected to be used for years, but leasing may be better for rapidly evolving imaging and diagnostic technology. Include training and software update costs in your ROI model.

Restaurants and hospitality

Kitchen systems, refrigeration, POS hardware, and prep equipment can produce immediate efficiency gains, but downtime risk is significant. Financing decisions should account for service contracts, replacement parts availability, and install timeline. If opening a new location, stagger financing so every asset does not reach end-of-life at the same time.

Manufacturing and fabrication

For CNC equipment and production lines, lenders often review residual value and expected resale market closely. Borrowers should map term length to throughput assumptions and customer concentration risk. A conservative model that still cash-flows under lower utilization makes approvals easier and prevents covenant pressure later.

Practical negotiation checklist before signing

  • Ask for a full fee schedule in writing: documentation, UCC filing, interim rent, and purchase option terms.
  • Clarify payment start date: especially if equipment delivery and installation are delayed.
  • Confirm end-of-term notice windows: missing a notice deadline can trigger unwanted auto-renewals.
  • Review insurance minimums: required limits can affect total carrying cost more than expected.
  • Negotiate cure periods: understand what happens after a missed payment and how quickly defaults escalate.

Final thoughts

Equipment financing can be a growth accelerator when terms match asset life, cash flow, and replacement strategy. In 2026, the smartest borrowers focus on all-in economics, operational impact, and contract clarity—not just headline rates. Build a clear use case, gather complete documentation, compare lenders like Balboa Capital, Crest Capital, and Taycor Financial, and negotiate for structure that supports resilience as well as growth.
