10 Business Loan Options for Startups With No Revenue

May 7, 2025 • 10 min read • Business Funding
Advertisement

Securing funding for a startup with limited or no revenue presents a significant challenge for entrepreneurs. Traditional lenders typically require a business history and consistent cash flow, leaving many early-stage ventures struggling to find capital. However, numerous financing alternatives exist specifically designed for pre-revenue startups.

In this comprehensive guide, we explore ten viable funding options for entrepreneurs at the earliest stages of business development, focusing on solutions that don't require established revenue streams.

1. SBA Microloans

The Small Business Administration (SBA) Microloan program provides small, short-term loans to startups and small businesses through community-based intermediary lenders. These loans are particularly suitable for early-stage businesses without established revenue.

  • Loan amounts: Up to $50,000 (average around $13,000)
  • Terms: Up to six years
  • Interest rates: Generally 8% to 13%
  • Eligibility: Small businesses in early stages, including pre-revenue

The microloan program places less emphasis on financial history and more on your business plan, market opportunity, and personal commitment. Many microlenders also provide valuable business training and technical assistance to borrowers, which can be particularly beneficial for first-time entrepreneurs.

2. Angel Investors

Angel investors are high-net-worth individuals who provide capital for startups, typically in exchange for ownership equity or convertible debt. Unlike traditional lenders, angel investors are willing to fund pre-revenue businesses based on potential rather than historical performance.

Key considerations for angel investment include:

  • Investment size: Typically $25,000 to $100,000 per angel, though angel groups may invest more
  • Value beyond money: Angels often provide mentorship, industry expertise, and valuable connections
  • Equity expectations: Generally 10% to 30% ownership stake, depending on valuation and investment amount
  • Focus areas: Many angels specialize in specific industries or business models

Finding the right angel investor involves networking through entrepreneurial communities, attending investor events, and utilizing platforms like AngelList or Gust. The ideal angel brings not just capital but relevant expertise and connections in your industry.

3. Crowdfunding Platforms

Crowdfunding allows entrepreneurs to raise small amounts from many individuals, often before generating revenue. There are several types of crowdfunding models:

  • Reward-based crowdfunding: Backers receive products or perks (e.g., Kickstarter, Indiegogo)
  • Equity crowdfunding: Investors receive company shares (e.g., StartEngine, Republic)
  • Debt crowdfunding: Lenders receive interest on their investment (e.g., Kiva, Funding Circle)
  • Donation-based crowdfunding: Supporters contribute without expecting financial returns (e.g., GoFundMe)

Successful crowdfunding campaigns require compelling storytelling, effective marketing, and a strong value proposition. Beyond raising capital, crowdfunding can validate your business concept and build an early customer base.

4. Small Business Grants

Unlike loans, grants provide funding that doesn't need to be repaid, making them an attractive option for pre-revenue startups. Grant sources include:

  • Federal grants: Programs like SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research) and STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer)
  • State and local grants: Economic development initiatives at regional levels
  • Corporate grants: Programs from large companies supporting innovation (e.g., FedEx Small Business Grant, Visa Everywhere Initiative)
  • Foundation grants: Private foundations supporting specific industries or causes

Grant applications typically require detailed business plans, clear objectives, and alignment with the program's goals. While the application process can be rigorous, the non-dilutive nature of grant funding makes it worth pursuing.

5. Business Incubators and Accelerators

Business incubators and accelerators provide early-stage companies with resources, mentorship, and sometimes funding to accelerate growth. Many programs accept pre-revenue startups:

  • Incubators: Typically provide workspace, mentoring, and resources over an extended period
  • Accelerators: Offer more structured programs (usually 3-6 months) with mentorship, resources, and often seed funding in exchange for equity

Well-known programs like Y Combinator, Techstars, and 500 Startups have helped launch thousands of successful companies. Even those that don't provide direct funding offer invaluable connections to investors and potential customers.

6. Venture Debt for Early-Stage Startups

While traditional venture debt typically requires revenue, some specialized lenders offer venture debt to pre-revenue startups that have already raised equity funding. This option combines elements of venture capital and traditional debt:

  • Loan structure: Term loans or lines of credit with interest rates higher than bank loans but lower than equity costs
  • Warrants: Often includes warrants (options to purchase equity at a set price)
  • Requirements: Usually requires prior equity investment from reputable investors

Providers like Silicon Valley Bank, Espresso Capital, and TriplePoint Capital offer specialized venture debt products for startups at various stages, including some pre-revenue companies with strong investor backing.

7. Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)

CDFIs are private financial institutions dedicated to delivering responsible, affordable lending to help underserved communities join the economic mainstream. Many CDFIs offer programs specifically for startups with limited or no revenue:

  • Loan types: Microloans, small business loans, lines of credit
  • Amounts: Typically $500 to $250,000
  • Focus: Often prioritize minority-owned, women-owned, and businesses in underserved areas
  • Support: Usually provide extensive technical assistance and business education

CDFIs like Accion Opportunity Fund, Pacific Community Ventures, and Local Initiatives Support Corporation (LISC) have specific programs designed for early-stage entrepreneurs.

8. Personal Assets and Credit

While not ideal for everyone, many entrepreneurs initially fund their ventures through personal financial resources:

  • Personal savings: The most straightforward self-funding option
  • Home equity loans: Leveraging home value for business capital
  • Personal credit cards: Used judiciously for short-term business expenses
  • Retirement account rollovers: ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups) allows using retirement funds without penalties

These options offer control and simplicity but involve personal financial risk. If pursuing this route, maintain clear boundaries between personal and business finances and consider the potential impact on personal credit and financial security.

9. Friends and Family Funding

Friends and family often represent an accessible source of early capital for pre-revenue startups. This funding can be structured in several ways:

  • Loans: Formal agreements with clear terms for repayment
  • Equity investment: Offering ownership stakes in the company
  • Convertible notes: Debt that converts to equity during future financing rounds
  • Gift: No-strings-attached contributions

To avoid potential relationship damage, document all arrangements professionally, clarify expectations upfront, and communicate regularly about business progress. Consider using platforms like Able Lending or Kiva to formalize these arrangements.

10. Business Plan Competitions and Pitch Events

Business competitions offer opportunities to win non-dilutive funding while gaining visibility and feedback. Many focus specifically on early-stage ventures:

  • University competitions: Many colleges host events with substantial prizes (e.g., MIT $100K, Harvard New Venture Competition)
  • Industry-specific competitions: Focused on particular sectors or technologies
  • Regional competitions: Promoting economic development in specific areas
  • Global competitions: Larger events like the Chivas Venture or Cartier Women's Initiative

Beyond potential prize money, these events provide invaluable networking opportunities, mentorship, and visibility to potential investors and customers.

Conclusion: Creating a Strategic Funding Approach

For pre-revenue startups, the most effective funding strategy often combines multiple sources. Consider these principles when developing your approach:

  • Start lean: Minimize initial capital requirements through lean startup methodologies
  • Layer funding sources: Combine non-dilutive options (grants, competitions) with strategic equity investment
  • Align timing: Match funding sources to your startup's development stage
  • Build relationships early: Connect with potential funders before you need capital

Remember that funding is a means to an end, not the end itself. Focus on creating a viable business model and delivering customer value, which ultimately makes your startup attractive to any funding source.
